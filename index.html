📊 Chapter 1

The Lie of Averages

“The average hides the pain of every individual.” John Tukey — Data Visualization Pioneer
Lin Chuan · Confused Architect

"Stella, users are complaining about payment gateway slowness again in the group chat. But I checked the Prometheus dashboard — the average response time over the past hour is only around 110ms. It looks healthy! Why is everyone complaining?"

Su Ran · Calm Mentor

"Lin Chuan, imagine this: 99 user requests have a latency of 10ms, but 1 request gets stuck in the background for 10 seconds. What's the average?"

Lin Chuan did the math: "About 109ms."

"Exactly. But for that user stuck for 10 seconds, that's a 100% outage experience. The average averages out 99 people's happiness and 1 person's pain, making you think everyone is happy."

Lin Chuan was stunned. He had never thought about it this way — data isn't a pile of cold numbers, it's the real experience of living people.

📌 Lesson 1: Distribution Matters More Than Average Core Insight

Su Ran drew a curve on the whiteboard with a long tail stretching to the right.

"System response times, throughput — these metrics are almost never symmetrical in the real world. Most requests are fast, but there are always some — due to GC, network jitter, lock contention — that are painfully slow. This is called a right-skewed distribution. And the average is precisely the most unreliable metric for such distributions."

🧠 Statistical Thinking · Three Measures of Central Tendency

  • Mean: The arithmetic average. It's like a judge easily swayed — one extreme value can make it deliver a completely different verdict.
  • Median: The middle value after sorting. It's the true "people's representative" — unaffected by a few extreme cases, telling you what at least 50% of users experience.
  • Mode: The most frequent value. It tells you what most people actually feel.
Lin Chuan · Beginning to Awaken

He was silent for a long time, then said: "So, my monitoring for the past three years — I've been looking at a carefully packaged lie?"

Su Ran didn't answer, just nodded gently.

Lin Chuan took a deep breath and opened RStudio. "Alright, starting today, let the data tell me the truth."

📌 第一课：分布比平均值更重要 核心洞察

苏然在白板上画了一条曲线，右端拖着一个长长的尾巴。

“系统响应时间、吞吐量——这些数据在现实世界中几乎从来不是对称的。大部分请求都极快，但总有一些请求——因为 GC、网络抖动、锁争用——会慢得离谱。这种分布叫做右偏态分布。而平均值，恰恰是这种分布中最不可靠的指标。”

🧠 统计思维 · 三大集中趋势指标

  • 均值 (Mean)：所有数的算术平均。它就像一个容易被带偏的法官——一个极端值就能让它说出完全相反的判决。
  • 中位数 (Median)：排序后位于中间的值。它是真正的“人民代表”——不受少数极端情况影响，告诉你 至少 50% 的用户体验如何。
  • 众数 (Mode)：出现最频繁的值。它告诉你 大多数人的真实感受。

The Secret Language of Traffic

“Randomness is not chaos, but another kind of order.” Jacob Bernoulli — Probability Pioneer
Su Ran · Mentor

"Shawn, we need to do the next phase of load testing. But before we start, I have a question: If you test with a fixed rate of 100 requests per second, can you simulate a real production environment?"

Lin Chuan · Confused

"Can't I? 100 requests per second — isn't that standard?"

Su Ran · Mentor

"Real user requests never arrive uniformly. They're like raindrops — sometimes a drizzle, sometimes a downpour. If you load test at a fixed rate, you'll never measure the connection pool's true behavior under peak load."

📌 Lesson 2: Probability Is the Engineer's Invisible Tool Core Insight

Poisson Distribution · Event Counting — The probability of an event occurring how many times in a fixed time interval.
💡 Example: "What's the probability that the gateway receives 47 requests in the next second?"
📐 Poisson Distribution · Probability Mass Function \[ P(X = k) = \frac{\lambda^k e^{-\lambda}}{k!}, \quad k = 0, 1, 2, \dots \]
P(X = k) = λᵏ · e⁻λ / k! , k = 0, 1, 2, ...
Exponential Distribution · Event Intervals — The probability of how long between two events.
💡 Example: "How many milliseconds until the next request arrives?"
📐 Exponential Distribution · Probability Density Function \[ f(t) = \lambda e^{-\lambda t} \quad (t \ge 0) \]
f(t) = λ · e⁻λᵗ , t ≥ 0
💻 R in Action · Simulating Real Traffic
# Average of 50 requests per second
lambda_val <- 50

# Simulate inter-arrival times for 1000 requests (exponential distribution)
inter_arrival_times <- rexp(1000, rate = lambda_val)

# Calculate arrival timestamps
arrival_timestamps <- cumsum(inter_arrival_times)

# See the natural QPS fluctuation — this is the real world
qps_per_second <- table(cut(arrival_timestamps, breaks = 0:20))
barplot(qps_per_second, col = "darkseagreen", 
        main = "Real-world QPS Fluctuation", 
        xlab = "Time (seconds)", ylab = "Requests")
“随机不是混乱，而是另一种秩序。” 雅各布·伯努利 (Jacob Bernoulli)

📌 第二课：概率是工程师的隐形工具 核心洞察

泊松分布 · 事件计数 —— 在固定时间间隔内，事件发生 多少次 的概率。
💡 例如：“网关在下一秒内收到 47 个请求”的概率有多大？
📐 泊松分布 · 概率质量函数 \[ P(X = k) = \frac{\lambda^k e^{-\lambda}}{k!}, \quad k = 0, 1, 2, \dots \]
P(X = k) = λᵏ · e⁻λ / k! , k = 0, 1, 2, ...
指数分布 · 事件间隔 —— 两次事件之间 相隔多久 的概率。
💡 例如：“下一个请求还要等多少毫秒才来？”
📐 指数分布 · 概率密度函数 \[ f(t) = \lambda e^{-\lambda t} \quad (t \ge 0) \]
f(t) = λ · e⁻λᵗ , t ≥ 0
💻 R 实战 · 模拟真实流量发生器

# 模拟 1000 个请求之间的间隔时间（指数分布）

# 计算每个请求的到达时刻

# 看看 QPS 的自然波动 —— 这才是真实世界

barplot(qps_per_second, col = "darkseagreen", 
        main = "真实世界的 QPS 波动", 
        xlab = "时间 (秒)", ylab = "请求数")

Seeing the Whole from the Part

“The sample is the only bridge to the population.” William Gosset — Inventor of the t-test
Lin Chuan · Engineering Dilemma

"Stella, production logs are several terabytes per day. Pulling everything to calculate means and percentiles kills disk and CPU. I want to sample just 100 entries per hour to represent millions of requests — but can I trust these 100 entries?"

Su Ran · Mentor

"This is where we invoke statistics' crown jewel — the Central Limit Theorem (CLT). It's the mathematical guarantee that lets you infer the whole world from a small sample."

Figure: The Central Limit Theorem — No matter how skewed the population, the sample means will always approximate a normal distribution

📌 Lesson 3: Seeing the Whole with Samples Core Insight

📐 Standard Error · Your Margin of Error \[ SE = \frac{s}{\sqrt{n}} \]
SE = s / √n
💻 R in Action · Lightweight Sampling + Confidence Intervals
# Million-level population
population_latency <- c(rnorm(1000000, mean = 120, sd = 15))

# Sample just 100 entries — enough? CLT says: Yes
set.seed(123)
sample_data <- sample(population_latency, size = 100)
sample_mean <- mean(sample_data)
sample_sd <- sd(sample_data)

# 95% Confidence Interval — your margin of error
n <- length(sample_data)
se <- sample_sd / sqrt(n)
margin_error <- qt(0.975, df = n - 1) * se

ci_lower <- sample_mean - margin_error
ci_upper <- sample_mean + margin_error

cat(sprintf("Sample mean: %.2f ms\n", sample_mean))
cat(sprintf("95%% Confidence Interval: [%.2f, %.2f] ms\n", ci_lower, ci_upper))
“样本是通往总体的唯一桥梁。” 威廉·戈塞特 (William Gosset) — t检验发明者
林川 · 工程困境

“Stella，生产环境的日志每天几个 TB。全量捞出来计算均值和百分位数，磁盘和 CPU 都撑不住。我想每小时只抽样 100 条，用它来代表这一个小时数百万请求的情况——但这 100 条能信吗？”

苏然 · 导师

图：中央极限定理 —— 无论总体多么扭曲，样本均值总会趋于正态

📌 第三课：用样本看见全貌 核心洞察

📐 标准误 · 你的误差范围 \[ SE = \frac{s}{\sqrt{n}} \]
SE = s / √n
💻 R 实战 · 轻量级抽样 + 置信区间

The Trap of Experiments

“Significance is not proof of truth, but an alarm of signal.” Ronald Fisher — Father of Modern Statistics
Lin Chuan · Architect Pushed to Deploy

"Stella, the product manager says the new version has a 2.1% conversion lift, with a \(p\)-value of 0.048, less than 0.05, and wants a full rollout. But the server error rate seems to be rising too. Should I trust the p-value?"

Su Ran · Cautious Mentor

"Is he checking the \(p\)-value every day? And stopping the experiment as soon as it drops below 0.05?"

Lin Chuan was startled: "Yes! How did you know?"

"Because this is classic p-hacking," Su Ran sighed, "p-values fluctuate randomly during experiments like stock prices. If you check daily and stop early, your error rate balloons from 5% to over 30%. This isn't science — it's gambling."

📌 Lesson 4: Hypothesis Testing Is a Trial, Not a Lottery Core Insight

⚖️ Hypothesis Testing · Presumption of Innocence

  • Null Hypothesis \(H_0\): The new version and the old version have no difference.
  • Alternative Hypothesis \(H_1\): The new version and the old version have a difference.
💻 R in Action · Rejecting p-hacking
# Real experimental data (not prematurely stopped)
control <- rnorm(500, mean = 0.12, sd = 0.02)
treatment <- rnorm(500, mean = 0.122, sd = 0.02)

# Proper two-sample t-test
t.test(treatment, control, alternative = "two.sided")
# p = 0.38 — not significant! The improvement is just random fluctuation
“显著性不是真相的证明，而是信号的警报。” 罗纳德·费希尔 (Ronald Fisher)
林川 · 被催着上线的架构师

“Stella，产品经理跑来说新版本转化率提升了 2.1%，\(p\) 值是 0.048，小于 0.05，要求全量上线。但服务器报错率好像也在上升。我该信 p 值吗？”

苏然 · 警惕的导师

“他是不是每天都在后台看 \(p\) 值？一旦看到低于 0.05 就立刻叫停？”

林川一惊：“对！你怎么知道？”

“因为这是经典的 p-hacking，”苏然叹了口气，“p 值在实验中会像股票一样随机波动。如果你每天查看并随时终止，你犯错的概率会从 5% 飙升到 30% 以上。这不是科学，这是赌博。”

📌 第四课：假设检验是审判，不是彩票 核心洞察

⚖️ 假设检验 · 无罪推定

  • 原假设 \(H_0\)：新版和旧版 没有 区别。
  • 备择假设 \(H_1\)：新版和旧版 有 区别。
💻 R 实战 · 拒绝 p-hacking
# 真实的实验数据（没有被提前终止）
control <- rnorm(500, mean = 0.12, sd = 0.02)
treatment <- rnorm(500, mean = 0.122, sd = 0.02)

# 科学的两样本 t 检验
t.test(treatment, control, alternative = "two.sided")
# p = 0.38 —— 不显著！新方案的提升只是随机波动
⚔️ Chapter 5

The Battle of Many

“When comparing multiple groups, you need one sword, not three knives.” John Tukey — Inventor of Tukey HSD
Lin Chuan · Architect Tortured by Meetings

"Stella, three caching strategies are causing heated debates in the meeting room. I want to run three pairwise \(t\)-tests — A vs B, B vs C, A vs C — whoever's fastest wins. Is that okay?"

Su Ran · Frustrated Mentor

"Absolutely not." Su Ran's voice was firm. "Each \(t\)-test has a 5% false positive rate. Run 3, and the overall error rate becomes \(1 - (1 - 0.05)^3 \approx 14.3\%\). Run 10, and it hits 40%. You're shooting with a shotgun — you'll hit something eventually, but that's luck, not truth."

📌 Lesson 5: ANOVA — A Global Judgment Core Insight

📐 F-Ratio · Signal vs. Noise \[ F = \frac{\text{Mean Square Between (MSB)}}{\text{Mean Square Within (MSW)}} \]
F = Mean Square Between / Mean Square Within
💻 R in Action · Strategy Showdown
set.seed(99)
A <- rnorm(50, mean = 45, sd = 5)
B <- rnorm(50, mean = 43, sd = 5)
C <- rnorm(50, mean = 35, sd = 5)  # C is clearly better

perf <- data.frame(
  Latency = c(A, B, C),
  Strategy = factor(rep(c("A", "B", "C"), each = 50))
)

anova_res <- aov(Latency ~ Strategy, data = perf)
summary(anova_res)  # p < 0.001 — the three groups are indeed different

TukeyHSD(anova_res)  # C-A and C-B are highly significant (p < 0.001)
“比较多个群体时，你需要一把剑，而不是三把小刀。” 约翰·图基 (John Tukey)
林川 · 被会议折磨的架构师

“Stella，三套缓存策略在会议室里吵翻天了。我想做三次两两 \(t\) 检验——A vs B, B vs C, A vs C——谁快用谁，行不行？”

苏然 · 无奈的导师

“绝对不行。”苏然的声音斩钉截铁，“每一次 \(t\) 检验都有 5% 的假阳性概率。做 3 次，整体犯错率就变成 \(1 - (1 - 0.05)^3 \approx 14.3\%\)。做 10 次，直奔 40%。你这是在用散弹枪打靶——总会有一次命中，但那是运气，不是真相。”

📌 第五课：方差分析 · 全局审判 核心洞察

📐 F 比率 · 信号 vs 噪音 \[ F = \frac{\text{组间均方 (MSB)}}{\text{组内均方 (MSW)}} \]
F = 组间均方 / 组内均方
💻 R 实战 · 策略对决

B <- rnorm(50, mean = 43, sd = 5)
C <- rnorm(50, mean = 35, sd = 5)  # C 明显更优

  Latency = c(A, B, C),
  Strategy = factor(rep(c("A", "B", "C"), each = 50))

📌 Lesson 7: Drawing the Line of Causality Core Insight

📐 Linear Regression Model \[ Y = \beta_0 + \beta_1 X + \epsilon \]
Y = β₀ + β₁·X + ε
Coefficient of Determination \(R^2\) — How much of the variation does the model explain?
💡 If \(R^2 = 0.85\), concurrency alone explains 85% of memory variation. That's an excellent model!
💻 R in Action · Setting Circuit Breaker Thresholds
set.seed(77)
requests <- runif(100, 1000, 10000)
memory <- 20 + 0.007 * requests + rnorm(100, 0, 4)

model <- lm(memory ~ requests)

# Predict memory usage at 8500 concurrent requests

predict(model, newdata = new_data, interval = "prediction")
# 95% prediction interval upper bound: 88.2%

🕳️ Chapter 8

The Betrayal of Accomplices

“Multicollinearity is the betrayal of the model — you think you're strengthening, but you're weakening.” David Kleinbaum — Epidemiologist and Statistician
Lin Chuan · Overconfident Architect

"Stella, to make the model more accurate, I stuffed in every monitoring dimension — concurrency, threads, CPU, GC frequency. But the model crashed — the p-value for concurrency became 0.45!"

Su Ran · Mentor Seeing Through Everything

"You've been ambushed by multicollinearity. When two variables are highly correlated — like concurrency and threads, which grow proportionally — the model can't determine which one is truly contributing predictive power. It's like hiring two identical programmers — you can't tell who wrote the good code."

📌 Lesson 8: The Courage to Cut Redundancy Core Insight

📐 Variance Inflation Factor \[ VIF_i = \frac{1}{1 - R_i^2} \]
VIFᵢ = 1 / (1 - Rᵢ²)
  • \(VIF > 5\): 🟡 Significant multicollinearity concern
  • \(VIF > 10\): 🔴 Severe multicollinearity — must remove!
💻 R in Action · Uncovering the Accomplice
set.seed(66)
req <- runif(100, 1000, 10000)
threads <- req * 0.05 + rnorm(100, 0, 10)  # Highly correlated with req!
cpu <- req * 0.009 + rnorm(100, 0, 2)     # Also highly correlated with req!
gc <- runif(100, 1, 20)                   # Independent variable

memory <- 15 + 0.005 * req + 0.3 * gc + rnorm(100, 0, 3)
df <- data.frame(memory, req, threads, cpu, gc)

bad_model <- lm(memory ~ req + threads + cpu + gc, data = df)
summary(bad_model)  # req's p-value becomes insignificant

library(car)
vif(bad_model)  # req and threads have VIF > 40!
“多重共线性是模型的背叛——你以为在增强，其实在削弱。” 戴维·克莱因鲍姆 (David Kleinbaum)

📌 Lesson 9: Never Trust an Unvalidated Model Core Insight

⚖️ The LINE Rules · The Model's Four Defenses

  • Linearity — There is indeed a linear relationship between dependent and independent variables
  • Independence — Residuals are independent, with no autocorrelation
  • Normality — Residuals follow a normal distribution with mean 0
  • Equal Variance — Residual variance does not change with independent variables
